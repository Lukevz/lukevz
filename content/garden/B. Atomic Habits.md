# The Fundamentals
## Chapter 1
Small choices compound

“Be more concerned with your current trajectory than your current results”

“ it makes no sense to restrict your satisfaction one scenario when there are many paths success￼”

You can be happy because your system is running not if you reached the original goals.

“ You do not rise to the level of your goals, you fall to the level of your systems.”￼

## Chapter 2
3 levels of change
1. Outcomes (what you get)
2. Process (what you do)
3. Identity (what you believe)

The deepest layer is your identity.

Might start a habit because of motivation, but you’ll stick with it because it becomes part of your identity

The Latin root of "identity" is **idem**, meaning "the same," which formed the Medieval Latin noun *identitas* (sameness), eventually giving us the English word. It signifies oneness, sameness, or the state of being the same thing or person over time, drawing from the core concept of *idem* ("the same") and potentially influenced by ==identidem("repeatedly") and essentitas ("being"==

“The process of building habits is the process of **becoming** yourself”

how to change your identity
1. Decide who you want to become
2. Prove it to yourself with evidence (habits)

## Chapter 3
“Habits are reliable solutions to recurring problems in our environment”

“Habits don’t restrict freedom they create it” - similar to Ali Abdaal [[B. Feel-Good Productivity]]

Process of building a habit 
1. Cue (trigger)
2. Craving (motivation)
3. ￼Response (action based on ability and craving)
4. Reward (end goal)

**For good habits**
Cue - make it obvious
Craving - make it attractive
Response - make it easy
Reward - make it satisfying

**For bad habits**
Inverse

# The First Law: Make it Obvious

1. Fill habit scorecard
2. Implementation intentions
3. Use habit stacking
4. Design your environment
5. Reduce exposure

## Chapter 4
Habits scorecard
- list your activities routine
- Rank as positive, neutral, and negative
- Acknowledge your behavior don’t judge yourself

## Chapter 5
- Be specific, measurable and planned
- Give habits a time and space

Habit Stacking
- Leverage current habits momentum to enable new habits
- Formula: After ==current habit==, I will ==new habit==
- Current habit + new habits = **routine stack**

Find the right trigger
- Write your routine in one column
- List all the things that happen to you without fail

Habits must include specific instruction and be bound to a clear cue.
*“When I close my laptop for lunch, I will do 10 pushups next to my desk”*

## Chapter 6
Environment often matters more than motivation

- make the cue a big part of your environment
- Be the designer of your world, not the consumer —> Remove bad cues, add positive ones
- Behavior is defined by our ==relationship to the objects== in our environment, they can also be ==linked to specific contexts==
  - It’s easier to form new habits in new environments
  - Avoid mixing contexts, one room per use
- Mixing contexts leads to mixing habits, where easier habits will often win out - which are typically the ones you already have
- Stable, predictable environment = stable, predictable habits

## Chapter 7
- Remove exposure to cues that trigger bad habits
  - “No one can maintain positive habits in a bad environment”
- Make it invisible, it’s easier to avoid temptation than resist it

# The Second Law: Make It Attractive

## Chapter 8
We have more chemistry for wanting than liking 

[[Temptation bundling]] - pairing something we need to do with something we want to do.

The more attractive an opportunity it is the more likely it will become habit forming

Habits are dopamine driven feedback loop

It is the anticipation not the fulfillment that causes us to act

We imitate the behavior of the close the many the powerful

## Chapter 9
We tend to adopt it happens to those around us.
Most days we would rather be wrong in a crowd than Wright alone.

## Chapter 10
Our behavior is closely tied to predicted outcomes and what we think will happen
Cues can shape the craving

Create a motivation ritual – do something you want to do before a difficult habit

# The Third Law: Make It East
## Chapter 11
"The best is the enemy of good”

Motion vs Action
- Motion is preparation but can be misinterpreted as action or a way to delay failure
- Only action can produce an outcome

## Chapter 12
Make it as easy as possible in the moment to do the things that pay off in the long run

[[The Law of Least Effort]]

Addition by subtraction: remove friction and add habits to existing paths (I.e. stopping at the gym on the way to work)

Prime your environment so it’s ready for immediate use
- Permanent (keep a box of scrapbooking with all you need in one place)
- Temporary (prep gym clothes the night before)

## Chapter 13
Little choices stack up and limit your future options. They are the entry point

[[The Two Minute Rule]] - when you start a new habit it should take less than 2 minutes to do. It must be easy to start

- Map out goals from very easy to very hard then identify the ==gateway habit==
- Make the first action effortless to build momentum

Standardize before you optimize
- Stop while things are going well, you can improve after you’ve mastered showing up
- Separate a habit goal into phases that are increasingly more advanced but start as simple as possible

## Chapter 14
Commitment devices can help making a choice in the present to hold you accountable in the future (I.e. smart plug for the router turns off at night)

- `I don’t like the sentiment that you don’t you can’t be trusted and be disciplined BUT if it’s done in the spirit of adding intentional friction it makes sense`

Make bad behavior impossible and good habits inevitable
- Add and remove friction by automating and taking one time actions that help you work on autopilot
- Examples: autopay, removing social media from your phone
- Let technology remember

# The Fourth Law: Make It Satisfying
## Chapter 15
Positive emotions cultivate habits, negative emotions destroy them

Humans weren’t built for delayed dopamine returns. Our reward centers are roughly the same size as our ancestors 200,000 years ago

The cost of good habits are in the present, the cost of bad ones is in the future
- the more immediate the gratification you get the more you should question it

**Cardinal rule**: what is immediately rewarded is repeated, what is immediately punished is avoided

## Chapter 16

It’s rewarding to see your progress stack up and can be critical during the bad days to help you stay on track.

Habit trackers help reinforce that feeling but perfection is impossible so when a streak is broken, the key is to hop right onto the next one – don’t skip twice a rule.

We optimize for what we measure, but it doesn’t always tell the whole story. Just because it’s measurable doesn’t mean it’s the most important and just because it’s not something you can track doesn’t mean it’s unimportant.

## Chapter 17
The Habit Contract - a verbal / written agreement to your habit outlining the punishment if you fail. Get an accountability partner to sign

`I like the formality of documenting your goal like this and going to the practice of building a roadmap and clearly defining what it is you wanna achieve. But I don't like the lack of grace and trust that is signaled at least when it's taken to the extreme of harsh, punishments and paying accountability partners because you failed ￼`

## Chapter 19
[[The Goldilocks Rule]] - peak motivation comes when working on tasks with “just manageable” difficult

Behaviors need to remain novel, the biggest threat to success is boredom

## Chapter 20
Once a behavior is automatic, you may stop paying attention to whether it is still effective or aligned with your goals.

Progress comes from reflection, review, and small refinements, not from blind repetition. You must periodically step back, evaluate your habits, and consciously improve them.

Two types of reviews
1. [Annual Reviews](https://jamesclear.com/annual-review)
   1. What went well this year?
   2. What didn’t go so well this year?
   3. What am I working toward?
2. [Integrity Report](https://jamesclear.com/integrity)
   1. What are the core values that drive my life and work?
   2. How am I living and working with integrity right now?
   3. How can I set a higher standard in the future?
